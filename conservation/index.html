The World's Most Trafficked Mammal

~100,000 pangolins are estimated to be illegally trafficked every year — more than any other wild mammal on Earth.

All eight species of pangolin are listed as threatened on the IUCN Red List, and their populations are declining across the board. The primary driver is illegal wildlife trade: pangolin scales are in high demand in parts of East and Southeast Asia, where they are used in traditional medicine despite no scientific evidence that they have any medicinal effect. Pangolin meat is also consumed as a luxury food in some regions.

The scale of the trade is staggering. Over a million pangolins are estimated to have been trafficked in the decade between 2008 and 2019 alone. In 2016, all eight species were moved to Appendix I of CITES — the highest level of international trade protection — banning commercial cross-border trade entirely. Despite this, trafficking continues largely unabated.

Key Threats

Illegal Wildlife Trade

The illegal trade in pangolins is driven primarily by demand for their scales, which are keratin — chemically identical to human fingernails — yet falsely believed to treat conditions ranging from arthritis to cancer. There is no peer-reviewed scientific evidence to support any of these claims. Pangolin meat is also sold as a luxury item in some markets. Both the scales and meat command very high black-market prices, making pangolins an extremely lucrative target for poachers and traffickers.

The trade is a global, organized criminal enterprise. Wildlife monitoring group TRAFFIC has identified over 159 smuggling routes used by pangolin traffickers worldwide.

Habitat Loss

Deforestation for agriculture, logging, and urban expansion destroys and fragments the habitats pangolins depend on. Fragmented populations are smaller, more isolated, and more vulnerable to local extinction — and fragmented forest makes it easier for poachers to access animals. In West Africa, conversion of forests to palm oil plantations has been particularly damaging.

Difficulty in Captive Breeding

Pangolins are notoriously difficult to keep and breed in captivity. They have highly specialized dietary requirements, are prone to stress-related illness, and have suppressed immune systems due to a genetic anomaly. Most pangolins rescued from traffickers have low survival rates, and captive breeding programs have had limited success — meaning wild population recovery depends almost entirely on reducing poaching pressure.

How You Can Help

Pangolins need advocates. Here are concrete things you can do to help:

  • Spread awareness — most people have never heard of a pangolin. Sharing what you know genuinely matters.
  • Donate to organizations that fund anti-poaching patrols, community education, and wildlife rehabilitation.
  • Support sustainable supply chains — avoid products linked to deforestation in pangolin habitats (palm oil, unsustainable timber).
  • Report suspicious wildlife products — if you see scales or pangolin products being sold, report it to local wildlife authorities or TRAFFIC.

Organizations Working to Save Pangolins

  • Save Pangolins A nonprofit dedicated entirely to pangolin conservation through research, education, and anti-poaching support.
  • World Wildlife Fund (WWF) Funds field research, works with governments to strengthen wildlife laws, and raises global awareness.
  • TRAFFIC Monitors and exposes illegal wildlife trade networks, working with law enforcement globally.
  • African Wildlife Foundation Works with local communities across Africa on habitat protection and anti-poaching programs.
  • IUCN Red List The global authority on species conservation status; tracks population trends for all eight pangolin species.
