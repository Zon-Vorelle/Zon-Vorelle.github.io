What is a Pangolin?

Pangolins are the only mammals on Earth whose bodies are covered in hard, overlapping scales. These scales are made of keratin — the same protein found in human fingernails and hair — and can make up nearly 20% of the animal's total body weight. Despite looking reptilian, pangolins are warm-blooded mammals belonging to the order Pholidota, whose name comes from the Greek for "clad in scales."

The name "pangolin" itself comes from the Malay word pengguling, meaning "one who rolls up" — a nod to their signature defensive posture. When threatened, a pangolin curls itself into a tight ball, with its scales forming an armored shell that even lions and leopards have difficulty breaking through.

Pangolins are solitary and almost entirely nocturnal. They are insectivores with no teeth, relying instead on an extraordinarily long, sticky tongue to capture ants and termites. Their closest living relatives — perhaps surprisingly — are carnivores like dogs, cats, and bears, not anteaters or armadillos, which they resemble through convergent evolution.

There are eight known living species of pangolin, split between Asia and sub-Saharan Africa. All eight are currently listed as threatened on the IUCN Red List, making pangolins one of the most imperiled groups of mammals on the planet.

The Eight Species

The eight pangolin species are divided into three genera. Asian species belong to Manis, while African species are split between Phataginus (small, tree-dwelling) and Smutsia (large, ground-dwelling).

Asian Species

Sunda Pangolin

Manis javanica

Found across Southeast Asia including Malaysia, Indonesia, and Singapore. Primarily tree-dwelling. Status: Critically Endangered.

Chinese Pangolin

Manis pentadactyla

Ranges from Nepal and northeastern India through southern China and Taiwan. Burrows in the ground. Status: Critically Endangered.

Indian Pangolin

Manis crassicaudata

Found across the Indian subcontinent. Larger than other Asian species with a longer tail. Status: Endangered.

Philippine Pangolin

Manis culionensis

Endemic to the Palawan island group in the Philippines. One of the smallest Asian species. Status: Critically Endangered.

African Species

White-bellied Pangolin

Phataginus tricuspis

The most common African pangolin; arboreal, found in central and west African forests. Status: Endangered.

Black-bellied Pangolin

Phataginus tetradactyla

Also called the long-tailed pangolin; uniquely active during the day. Lives in rainforest canopy. Status: Vulnerable.

Ground Pangolin

Smutsia temminckii

Also called Temminck's pangolin; found across eastern and southern Africa in savanna and woodland. Status: Vulnerable.

Giant Ground Pangolin

Smutsia gigantea

The largest of all eight species, reaching up to 1.4 m in length. Found in central and west African forests. Status: Endangered.

Fun Facts

  • A pangolin's tongue can be longer than its entire body — up to 40 cm — and is stored in its chest cavity when not in use.
  • Pangolins have no teeth. Instead they swallow small stones that sit in a muscular gizzard to grind up insects, much like a bird.
  • Their scales are not attached at the tips, which means they can slice like razor blades when a pangolin flexes — an extra deterrent for predators.
  • A single pangolin can consume over 70 million insects per year, making them a vital natural pest controller.
  • Baby pangolins, called pangopups, are born with soft, white scales that harden and darken within a few days. They ride on their mother's tail for up to three months.
  • The black-bellied pangolin is the only species known to be regularly active during daylight hours.
  • Pangolins have poor eyesight but an exceptional sense of smell, which they use to locate insect mounds while foraging at night.
  • Despite their armored appearance, pangolins are surprisingly good swimmers.
